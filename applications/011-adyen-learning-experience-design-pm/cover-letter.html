My bachelor thesis at KONGSBERG was on simulation-based learning: how engineers learn by doing, not by listening. That question has shaped every learning environment I have built since. The Beer Game at Windesheim, the Blockchain Minor at InHolland, the gamified startup simulations at ROC Midden Nederland — each one built on the same insight. People learn when the stakes feel real, the design is safe, and the feedback is immediate.

The full-stack LXD work is there across the career: needs assessment at the start (what do practitioners actually need, not what they think they need), design (simulation vs. course vs. workshop), build (interactive format and self-hosted tooling), delivery, and iteration. At WDS Global, I solved a 10,000-ticket support backlog not with more staff but with a tutorial video series that HTC later adopted globally as their standard customer education format. The problem was a learning design problem, not a capacity problem.
