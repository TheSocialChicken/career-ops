### [Stakeholder escalation / data governance] KONGSBERG — The BP Data Contract

**Source:** Direct — KONGSBERG internship / field role, HvA period
**S (Situation):** Field Service Engineer at KONGSBERG on active oil rig deployments (North Sea). No internet on the rig — fully self-reliant. Technically multi-disciplinary role: wiring, electronics, data translation, software installation, documentation, implementation, training. Parallel engagement: data translation and upkeep on a live system being upgraded. The data contract between GeoServices (service company) and BP (client) stated that BP had rights to their own operational data — but GeoServices was withholding it.
**T (Task):** Not formally my responsibility. But the deployment was stalling. Data couldn't flow. I recognized the problem was contractual, not technical.
**A (Action):** Spent 2 months working through normal channels — engineers, technical contacts, meetings, including an in-person office visit. They were polite and uncooperative. Realized this wouldn't move at technical level. Got hold of the actual contract. Read it. Confirmed GeoServices was in clear breach. Went directly to their CEO, presented the breach, and demanded compliance.
**R (Result):** They complied. Data contract honoured. Deployment unblocked.
**Reflection:** Diplomacy has a time limit. When you've given reasonable time and the problem is structural (a power dynamic, a financial incentive, a contract violation), escalation isn't aggression — it's the correct tool. The key was having the contract in hand: not opinion, not frustration, just the agreement they signed.
**Best for questions about:**
- "Tell me about a time you took initiative beyond your role"
- "Describe a conflict with an external party you resolved"
- "How do you handle uncooperative stakeholders?"
- "Tell me about a time you escalated a problem — and why"
- "Give me an example of working in a high-pressure or isolated environment"
- "How do you deal with blockers that others have given up on?"

### [Innovation lab / ecosystem building] Windesheim AI Lab — Knopenkoning + Value Chain Hackers

**Source:** Direct — Windesheim Hogeschool, current/recent engagement
**S (Situation):** Running the innovation lab function at Windesheim. Two connected projects: (1) Knopenkoning — AI-powered supply chain mapping tool, (2) Value Chain Hackers — the broader innovation lab and community around supply chain AI and compliance (CSRD/EUDR).
**T (Task):** Build practical AI tools that researchers and students can actually use, not just demo. Map supply chains intelligently. Make compliance (EUDR traceability, CSRD reporting) something that can be experienced and learned, not just lectured about.
**A (Action):** Built the AI stack (self-funded: Flowise, n8n, OpenWebUI, Supabase). Developed the Beer Game demonstrator — a live simulation showing blockchain vs. non-blockchain supply chain. Trained students and researchers to use the tools independently. Recruited students as interns into the VCH ecosystem.
**R (Result):** Live demonstrator in active university use. Cross-European supply chain lab. Students moving from classroom to real project work inside the lab.
**Reflection:** The simulation was the unlock. When people play the beer game and feel the bullwhip effect themselves, they understand supply chain risk in 20 minutes. No lecture achieves that.
**Best for questions about:**
- "Describe a project you built from scratch"
- "How do you make complex concepts accessible?"
- "Give an example of building a learning environment"
- "How do you use AI practically, not just theoretically?"
